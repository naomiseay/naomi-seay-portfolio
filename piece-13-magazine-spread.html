How Remote Work Reshaped the American Midwest

For decades, the story of the American Midwest was one of departure. Young professionals left for the coasts, talent drained from cities like Riverside, Millhaven, and Harlow City as the promise of Silicon Valley and Manhattan loomed large. But something unexpected happened when the world was forced to work from home: the people who had already left started coming back.

47%
of Riverside workers now fully remote

The shift has been profound. Riverside's waterfront district, once a symbol of urban blight now gentrified into a food and arts district, has become a microcosm of this new geography of work. Coffee shops fill with MacBooks. Co-working spaces operate at capacity. The city has added over 12,000 new residents since 2022, many of them remote workers drawing coastal salaries while paying Midwestern rents.

"We spent ten years watching our best people leave. Now they're calling us, asking if there's still room."
— Mayor Afi Brown, Millhaven, OH

The economic multiplier effects extend beyond the obvious. A remote worker earning a San Francisco-level salary and spending it in a mid-sized Midwest city is, in essence, an import-export engine. They import wages and export them into the local economy at outsized impact. Restaurants, contractors, daycare providers, and independent retailers all benefit from this new flow of capital. Economic development officials in Westport estimate the net effect has added nearly $400 million in annual consumer spending since 2023.
